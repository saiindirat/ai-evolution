An illustrated story in seven chapters

The Bagel That
Broke the Robot

How AI learned seven new tricks — and what it still gets wrong.

Computers did not wake up smart one morning. People kept teaching them new ways to solve problems — one breakthrough at a time.

Meet Maya and Dot. Maya is a curious 10-year-old. Dot is her robot helper. In every chapter, Dot learns one new trick that fixes a problem from the chapter before. Dot never becomes perfect — and that turns out to be the most interesting part.

One thing to keep in mind: AI does not think exactly like a person. It learns and uses patterns in data.

And yes — there is a real bagel. It shows up in chapter 1, defeats a machine with 2,500 carefully written rules, and then refuses to leave. Keep an eye on it.

Seventy years, very unevenly spread

The shape of this picture is the story: decades of slow, difficult work — then a rush.

The seven tricks · choose a chapter

These ideas overlap. The dates show important breakthrough moments — not the exact day each idea was invented.

Chapter 1Instructions1950s–1980s

Dot follows exact instructions

The problem

Maya wants toast. Dot has no idea what toast is. Dot knows nothing at all yet.

Dot's new trick

Maya writes down every single step. If the bread is in the slot, then push the lever. If two minutes have passed, then pop it up. Dot follows the list exactly.

Toast appears. Maya is delighted. Dot is delighted, in whatever way a robot is delighted.

Then Maya's dad puts a bagel in the slot.

Dot freezes. There is no rule about bagels. Dot does nothing at all while the bagel sits there, quietly not being toasted.

Break Dot's rules yourself

Dot only knows the toast rules Maya wrote. Put something in the toaster and see what happens.

Dot is waitingChoose something to put in the toaster.

How it works

People write the rules by hand. The computer checks each rule in turn. When a rule matches, it does what the rule says. When none match, nothing happens.

One real moment

1980. A program called XCON started helping a computer company work out which parts belonged in each customer's order. It grew to roughly 2,500 hand-written rules. For that one narrow job, it did well.

2,500If you thought up and wrote one new rule every single day, that's about seven years of writing. And you'd still be missing the bagel.

✓ What it could do

Handle narrow, predictable jobs very reliably. And it could always show you exactly which rule it used — so you could check its work.

✕ What it still got wrong

The real world is full of bagels. Every surprise needed a brand-new rule, and nobody could write them all. Worse, new rules kept breaking old ones.

Which leads to chapter 2

What if, instead of writing every rule, we showed Dot many examples?

Explore the real history

The phrase “artificial intelligence” was coined at a 1956 summer workshop at Dartmouth College, organised by John McCarthy and others.

Programs built this way were called expert systems. John McDermott at Carnegie Mellon began building XCON (also called R1) in 1978 for Digital Equipment Corporation; it went into regular use in 1980 and eventually held about 2,500 rules. Reported savings vary between roughly $10M and $40M a year — a good reminder that widely repeated business numbers are often estimates.

The deeper problem was maintenance. As rules piled up, changing one could break another in ways nobody predicted. Interest and funding rose and fell several times; researchers nicknamed the quiet stretches “AI winters.” Rule-based systems never disappeared — they still run inside plenty of software today. Chapter 2Examples1980s–2000s

Dot learns from examples

The problem

Maya's inbox is full of junk mail. She tries writing rules. If it says “free prize,” then it's junk. But the senders just change their words. Every rule she writes stops working by Friday.

The bagel problem again. Maya can't write a rule for every surprise — not for breakfast, and not for her inbox. So this time she doesn't try.

Dot's new trick

Maya stops writing rules. Instead she shows Dot 1,000 emails. She puts a label on each one: junk or not junk.

That's all she does. She never explains what junk mail looks like. Dot studies the labelled piles and works out the patterns itself.

How it works

The pile of labelled examples is called training data. Dot makes a guess about an email, checks it against the label, and adjusts. Do that thousands of times and the guesses get good.

One real moment

1989. Yann LeCun and colleagues at Bell Labs built a system that learned to read handwritten numbers from labelled examples. It was put to work reading postal codes, and later cheques. The famous practice set of digits, MNIST, holds 60,000 labelled training images.

60,000Label them yourself at five seconds each and you'd be there about 83 hours — two full working weeks, no breaks, just typing numbers you can already read at a glance.

Teach Dot “dog” — and watch it learn the wrong thing

Here are the photos Maya collected. Notice anything they have in common? Train Dot, then test it.

Not trained yetPress “Train Dot” to see what pattern it picks up.

No test yetTrain Dot first, then try a test photo.

What just happened

Dot learned whatever the examples actually shared — not what Maya meant them to share. Every dog photo had grass, so Dot decided grass means dog.

Dot isn't being stupid. It's being obedient. It found a pattern that worked perfectly on every example it was given. The problem was the examples.

This matters far beyond dogs. When systems like this are used to sort job applications or decide who gets a loan, and the examples carry old unfairness, Dot learns the unfairness and repeats it — quickly, at scale, and with total confidence.

The question every kid asks

“Why does Dot need 60,000 pictures? I learned what a dog was from seeing two.”

This is a genuinely good question, and researchers do not fully know the answer. But part of it is this: you weren't starting from zero.

By the time someone pointed at a dog, you already knew that objects stay whole when they move, that a thing far away is still the same size, that animals have fronts and backs, that faces matter. You'd been collecting that for two years. You also saw the dog in moving 3D from every angle — not one flat photo. And someone said the word out loud while you were looking.

Dot got 60,000 flat pictures and nothing else. No body, no years of watching things fall, nobody pointing. Closing that gap is one of the real open problems in AI.

✓ What it could do

Catch junk mail it had never seen before, because it learned the pattern instead of memorising the words. Rules could never do that.

✕ What it still got wrong

Dot only knows what the examples showed it. Bad examples teach the wrong pattern — and Dot can't tell you it learned “grass.” It just quietly gets things wrong later.

Which leads to chapter 3

Examples helped Dot learn simple patterns, but real pictures contained millions of tiny clues. How could Dot combine them?

Explore the real history

This family of methods is called machine learning. Learning from labelled examples is supervised learning — the label is the “supervision.”

Yann LeCun and colleagues applied backpropagation to handwritten digit recognition at Bell Labs starting in 1989; the refined LeNet-5 was published in 1998. Systems in this family were used commercially to read cheques.

The grass problem is real and has a name: spurious correlation, or more broadly bias in training data. It is not solved. It matters most when models are used to make decisions about people.

A myth worth correcting: IBM's Deep Blue beat world chess champion Garry Kasparov in 1997, and it's often told as a machine-learning story. It mostly wasn't. Deep Blue relied on custom hardware, searching enormous numbers of positions, and scoring rules hand-tuned with help from human chess masters — closer to chapter 1 than chapter 2. See sources ↓

Chapter 3Layersaround 2012

Dot works in layers, like a team of detectives

The problem

Maya shows Dot a photo of her cat. Dot fails. A photo isn't one clue — it's millions of tiny dots of colour. Which ones matter? Looking at them all at once is hopeless.

Dot's new trick

Dot stops working alone and builds a team of detectives, sitting in rows.

The first row only looks for tiny things: edges, corners, patches of colour. That's all they do. They pass their findings back.

The middle row combines those edges into shapes — a curve here, a pointed triangle there. The last row combines the shapes: two pointed triangles, two round eyes, whiskers. “Cat,” says Dot.

No single detective knows what a cat is. Together, they get there.

Meanwhile, in the kitchen

Maya fetches the bagel from chapter 1 and holds it up to the camera.

“Bagel,” says Dot.

Maya cheers. Nobody wrote a bagel rule. Nobody wrote any rule. Dot just looked — and after enough examples and enough layers, it knew.

How it works

Each row of detectives is a layer. Every layer passes what it found to the next one. Stack up enough layers, train them on lots of labelled pictures, and the whole team gets sharp.

People sometimes call this a “brain-like” system. It isn't a brain. It's maths arranged in rows, loosely inspired by an old idea about brain cells.

One real moment

2012. A yearly contest asked programs to name what was in photos, using a huge collection of labelled images called ImageNet. A layered system nicknamed AlexNet made an error only 15.3% of the time. The runner-up, built the old way, was about 26%. That gap changed the field almost overnight.

15.3%Out of 100 photos, AlexNet got about 15 wrong. The next-best got about 26 wrong. Eleven photos doesn't sound like much — but it was the difference between “interesting experiment” and “put it in your phone.”

✓ What it could do

Recognise objects in messy real photos far better than anything before — which is why your phone can find every picture of your dog. And why it finally caught the bagel.

✕ What it still got wrong

Dot spots patterns, but doesn't know what a cat is. Change a few dots of colour that a person wouldn't even notice, and Dot may confidently say “bus.”

Which leads to chapter 4

Dot became much better at pictures. But sentences were difficult, because words can affect other words far away.

Explore the real history

These layered systems are called neural networks; stacking many layers is deep learning. The “neural” name is historical — loosely inspired by biological neurons, not copies of them.

Fei-Fei Li and colleagues released ImageNet in 2009 — the huge labelled dataset that made the contest possible. In 2012 Alex Krizhevsky, with Ilya Sutskever and Geoffrey Hinton at Toronto, entered AlexNet, trained on ordinary gaming graphics chips (GPUs). Its top-5 error of 15.3% beat the runner-up's ~26%.

The idea was much older than 2012 and had a bumpy road. Frank Rosenblatt's Perceptron (1958) was an early single-layer version, hyped heavily in the press. A 1969 book by Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert set out clear limits of that design, and interest cooled for years. What eventually made deep networks practical was a mix of better training methods, far more data, and much faster chips — not one single insight.

Also worth knowing: in 2016, DeepMind's AlphaGo beat top Go player Lee Sedol 4–1. It combined layered networks with game-tree search and self-play, so it isn't the same recipe as image recognition — but it convinced many people that deep learning was serious. See sources ↓

Chapter 4Attention2017

Dot connects the important words

The problem

Dot can spot a bagel across the kitchen. So Maya leaves it a note:

“The dog chased the ball because it was rolling.”

Then she asks: what is it? Dot is stuck. Dot was reading one word at a time, and by the time it reached “it,” the word “ball” felt far away and long ago.

Odd, isn't it? Dot can recognise a bagel from a blurry photo, but can't work out what a three-letter word points at. Seeing and reading turned out to be different problems.

Dot's new trick

Dot stops reading in a straight line. For every word, Dot looks at the other words and decides which ones matter to that word.

For “it,” Dot compares two candidates: dog and ball. Which one rolls? Dot gives “ball” a strong link and “dog” a weak one. This trick is called attention.

How it works

Dot gives every pair of words a score for how much they matter to each other. High score, strong link. Because Dot checks the pairs together instead of in a queue, computers can do a lot of that work at the same time — which makes training much faster.

81That sentence has 9 words, so there are 9 × 9 = 81 pairs to score. Double the sentence to 18 words and it jumps to 324. This is why long documents are still hard: the work grows much faster than the text does.

One real moment

2017. Eight researchers at Google published a paper with a bold title: “Attention Is All You Need.” It introduced a design called the Transformer. Transformer-style designs power many of today's leading language models — the “T” in ChatGPT stands for Transformer.

✓ What it could do

Handle long sentences where distant words matter. Translate far better. And train quickly enough to learn from truly enormous amounts of text.

✕ What it still got wrong

Linking words is not the same as understanding them. Dot still trips over tricky sentences — and as you saw, every extra word costs it dearly.

Which leads to chapter 5

Once Dot became better at connecting words, it became surprisingly good at guessing what should come next.

Explore the real history

The paper was “Attention Is All You Need” (Vaswani et al., 2017), with eight authors, published while they were at Google. It was aimed at machine translation; its wider influence came later.

Attention wasn't brand new in 2017 — earlier translation systems already used a version of it. The Transformer's contribution was to drop the step-by-step recurrence and lean on attention, which made training far more parallel.

The 81-pairs point is real: standard self-attention cost grows with the square of the sequence length. A great deal of research since has gone into making that cheaper, which is why context windows keep getting longer.

Transformer-style architectures dominate language models today, but avoid absolutes: other designs exist and are actively researched, and many real systems mix approaches. See sources ↓

Chapter 5Creation2020–2022

Dot creates by predicting what comes next

The problem

Dot could sort things and connect words. But Maya wants a bedtime story. Sorting can't make a story. Dot has never made anything new.

So before we explain this one — play it yourself.

Be the predictor

Pick the word you think comes next. Then do it again. And again. That's the entire trick.

Round 1 of 5Pick a word to follow “Once upon a”.

Dot's new trick

That's it. That's the whole thing.

Dot has read an enormous amount of text, so it's very good at the guessing game. Guess a piece, add it, guess again. Repeat — very fast, thousands of times — and stories, summaries, answers, and computer code all come out.

Dot isn't reciting a story it has stored somewhere. It's guessing what fits next, over and over.

How it works

Dot works out which piece is likely to come next, adds it, and repeats.

Here's the catch, and it's the most important sentence on this page: likely-sounding is not the same as true. Dot is guessing what fits, not checking what's real.

Meanwhile, in the kitchen

Maya, curious, asks: “Dot, who invented the bagel?”

Dot answers instantly and warmly: “The bagel was invented in 1683 by a baker named Johann Kessler, to celebrate a famous horse ride.”

It sounds wonderful. The date is specific. The name has a ring to it. Maya nearly writes it down.

Dot made it up. Not to trick her — Dot was doing exactly its job, assembling words that fit. A confident name and a precise year are what usually comes next in a sentence like that. Fitting and true are not the same thing, and Dot cannot tell the difference.

Dot's half-trick: people teach it what “good” means

There's a step almost everyone skips, and it's the one that made any of this useful.

A pure next-word guesser is not a helpful assistant. Ask an untrained one “What is the capital of France?” and it may cheerfully continue with “What is the capital of Spain? What is the capital of Peru?” — because on the internet, questions are usually followed by more questions. It's guessing perfectly. It's just useless.

So people stepped in. Human workers wrote out good answers by hand. Then they were shown pairs of Dot's answers and asked, over and over: which of these two is better? Those judgements were used to teach Dot what a helpful answer looks like.

Every chatbot you have ever used was shaped by people sitting at desks, patiently rating answers. That's not a footnote to the story. It's the reason chapter 5 has a chapter 6 and 7 at all.

One real moment

30 November 2022. OpenAI put a simple chat box in front of one of these models and called it ChatGPT. Two things made it work: the model had been taught by human raters to actually answer questions, and anyone could now just type. Reports at the time estimated it reached about 100 million users in two months.

1.3B
beat
175BHere's the fact that should stop you. Researchers took a model with 175 billion parameters and one with 1.3 billion — 100 times smaller. The small one was trained with human feedback. Human raters preferred the small one's answers.

Being 100× bigger lost to being taught by people what “good” means.

(And for scale on “175 billion”: if you counted the parameters out loud, one per second, without ever sleeping, you'd finish in about 5,500 years.)

✓ What it could do

Write stories, explain ideas, summarise long text, draft code, and answer questions in ordinary language — helpfully, because people taught it what helpful means.

✕ What it still got wrong

Sometimes Dot invents an answer that sounds confident but is not true. People call these hallucinations. Dot won't look embarrassed. It sounds exactly as sure as when it's right — ask it about bagels and see.

Which leads to chapter 6

But the world is not made only of words. It also contains pictures, voices, music, and video.

Explore the real history

These are large language models (LLMs). The pieces they predict are tokens — roughly a word or part of a word. The dials adjusted while learning are parameters.

Two separate things get blurred together:

1. The scaling. OpenAI's GPT-3 (2020 paper) had 175 billion parameters, up from 117 million in GPT-1 (2018). Making models bigger, with more data and compute, kept improving them.

2. The human feedback. The technique is RLHF — reinforcement learning from human feedback. In OpenAI's InstructGPT work (Ouyang et al., 2022), labellers wrote example answers and ranked model outputs; those rankings trained a “reward model,” which was then used to fine-tune the language model. OpenAI reported that human evaluators preferred outputs from a 1.3B-parameter InstructGPT over the 175B GPT-3. OpenAI described ChatGPT as a sibling model to InstructGPT, trained with the same method.

3. The interface. ChatGPT launched 30 November 2022 as a “research preview.” The “100 million users in two months” figure comes from a UBS estimate reported by Reuters in February 2023 — an outside estimate, not an audited company number.

Hallucination isn't a bug someone forgot to fix. It follows from how the system works: it is built to produce likely text, and likely text is usually — but not always — true. RLHF reduces it. It does not remove it.

Worth sitting with: the people who do this rating work are usually contractors, often paid modestly, and some review genuinely distressing material so that models won't repeat it. They rarely appear in the story. See sources ↓

Chapter 6Many senses2023–2025

Dot combines different kinds of information

The problem

Maya's plant is going yellow. She could type a description — but she'd have to find the right words for a colour she can see perfectly well. Words-only Dot can't help with a thing you point at.

Dot's new trick

Maya holds up her phone, takes a photo of the drooping yellow leaves, and just asks out loud: “What's wrong with my plant?”

Dot takes in all three at once — the picture, her spoken question, and what it has read about plants — and answers: the leaves are yellow near the bottom and the soil looks wet, so this might be too much water.

How it works

A multimodal model can take in combinations of text, images, audio, and video. Each kind of input gets turned into numbers, and the model works with those patterns together.

Be careful with the word “senses.” Dot does not see the plant or hear Maya the way you do. It converts a photo and a sound into patterns, and finds connections between them.

One real moment

March 2023. OpenAI released GPT-4, which could accept both images and text as input and respond in text. Over the next two years, models handling several kinds of input at once became ordinary rather than remarkable.

The bagel, one last time. Maya's dad finally toasts it — and burns it. Maya photographs the wreckage and asks Dot what went wrong. Dot looks at the char, listens to her question, and suggests the setting was too high. Four chapters ago, this bagel made Dot freeze solid. It has come a long way. It still didn't smell the smoke.

✓ What it could do

Answer questions about a photo, read a chart you point at, follow a spoken question, or describe what's happening in a video.

✕ What it still got wrong

Dot misreads images and states wrong guesses confidently. And it only knows what's in the picture. It can't touch the soil, smell the smoke, or know Maya watered the plant twice this morning.

Which leads to chapter 7

Dot could understand and create many kinds of information, but it still waited for Maya to give every instruction.

Explore the real history

The word is multimodal — each “mode” is a kind of input: text, image, audio, video. Turning an input into a list of numbers the model can use is called creating an embedding.

GPT-4 was announced in March 2023 and accepted image and text inputs, returning text. Other labs shipped models with overlapping abilities around the same period; the timing is genuinely close, and “who was first” depends on which specific ability you count.

A separate note: in 2024, Geoffrey Hinton and John Hopfield shared the Nobel Prize in Physics for foundational work on machine learning with artificial neural networks — the ideas behind chapter 3, honoured decades later. A nice bit of history, but not part of the multimodal story. See sources ↓

Chapter 7Actions2025–2026

Dot makes a plan and uses tools

The problem

Maya's class picnic is on Saturday and there's a lot to sort out. Dot can answer any single question she asks. But Maya has to think of every question herself, one at a time, and do all the actual work.

Dot's new trick

Maya says one thing: “Help me plan the class picnic on Saturday.”

Dot makes a plan and works through it: check Saturday's weather, find three parks nearby with shade, write a packing list for 25 children, draft an invitation for their families.

(The packing list includes bagels. Of course it does.)

Then Dot stops and shows Maya the invitation. “Shall I send this?” Because sending it is Maya's decision, not Dot's.

How it works

An agent is a model connected to a plan, a memory, and tools. The model decides what to do next. The tools — a weather service, a map, a calendar — let it actually do things.

The memory matters: it's how Dot remembers, at step four, what it found at step one.

Notice what's underneath. Dot needed every earlier trick for this. Rules to follow a plan. Examples and layers to read a map. Attention and prediction to write the invitation. Many senses to check the sky. Seven tricks, all working at once, to organise one picnic.

Examples in 2026 as of 17 July 2026

Several companies now sell agents of this kind, and no one of them is the finish line. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Perplexity all ship agent products. As one dated example: Perplexity launched a product called Computer on 25 February 2026, which routes different parts of a task to different AI models — reported at launch as 19 of them.

How to read claims like these: “launched on this date” is verifiable. “Does the work of a whole team” is usually a company's own claim, and worth treating differently from an independent test. This field moves fast, so anything in this box may be out of date by the time you read it.

✓ What it could do

Carry out long jobs with many steps, using real tools, without being told each step — which saves a great deal of tedious work.

✕ What it still got wrong

Dot can misunderstand the goal, get stuck repeating itself, use a tool the wrong way, or finish confidently with a wrong answer. And a mistake now does something — a wrong email really gets sent. That's why step four exists.

One last question

Dot has seven tricks now. So who decides what Dot should do — and who checks whether it got it right?

Explore the real history

Giving a model tools is often called tool use or function calling. Letting it plan its own steps toward a goal makes it an agent. Some agents split work among smaller helpers, called sub-agents. Sending each part of a job to whichever model handles it best is orchestration.

Dated product facts (17 July 2026): Perplexity launched Computer on 25 February 2026; press coverage at launch described it as orchestrating 19 AI models. A version for legal teams, Computer for Counsel, was announced 24 June 2026 and described as drawing on more than 20 models. These are launch facts reported by the press.

Company claims vs. independent findings. Claims about how much work an agent replaces usually come from the companies selling them, and independent testing of agents is still early. Treat performance claims as claims until someone outside the company checks them.

After the story

Where things stand

Seven tricks in, here's an honest picture of what Dot — and every real AI system — can and can't do.

What AI is good at

  • Spotting patterns in huge piles of data
  • Producing a first draft of almost anything
  • Doing repetitive work without getting bored
  • Working in many languages at once
  • Being available at 3am when you're stuck

What AI still gets wrong

  • Inventing facts that sound convincing
  • Repeating unfairness that was in its examples
  • Sounding equally confident when right and wrong
  • Missing context nobody wrote down
  • Failing in ways that surprise its own builders

Why humans still matter

  • People choose the goal — Dot never picks the picnic
  • People check the work, because Dot can't tell when it's wrong
  • People taught it what “good” means in the first place
  • People carry responsibility when something goes badly
  • People decide what shouldn't be built at all

What AI costs

  • Computers: thousands of specialised chips
  • Electricity: a lot — data centres and their power use are now a live public debate
  • Data: huge amounts of text and images, raising real questions about who made it and who agreed
  • Human work: people label examples, rate answers, and review harmful content — often invisibly, as you saw in chapter 5
Industry snapshot (business context)

This part changes fastest and matters least for understanding how AI works — which is why it's tucked away here.

As of mid-2026, several AI companies carry valuations in the hundreds of billions of dollars, and large sums are being invested in data centres and chips. Exact figures move constantly and are often reported second-hand, so this page deliberately doesn't list them. For current numbers, go to a primary source — a company's own announcement or a regulatory filing — and check the date on it.

Be especially wary of market-size projections for years like 2030 or 2034. They are estimates produced by firms that sell reports, they vary wildly between firms, and they are not facts.

One genuine debate: whether AI keeps improving mainly by making models bigger, or whether new ideas are needed. Serious researchers disagree. Anyone who tells you it's settled is selling something.

Check yourself

Seven questions

No names, no dates, no numbers. These ask whether the ideas landed. Pick an answer to see why.

1. Why did one bagel defeat a program with 2,500 carefully written rules?

Exactly. It's not that bagels are hard — it's that nobody can list every situation ahead of time. That wall is what pushed people toward learning from examples.

2. Every dog photo Dot trains on happens to have grass in it. What might go wrong?

You made this happen yourself in chapter 2. Dot learns whatever the examples actually share — not what you meant them to share. This is why unbalanced training data is serious, especially when models make decisions about people.

3. In a layered system that recognises pictures, what do the early layers notice?

Early layers catch the small stuff. Middle layers build those into shapes. Later layers assemble shapes into an object. Like the detectives: nobody does the whole job, but the team gets there.

4. In “The dog chased the ball because it was rolling,” what does attention help the model compare?

Attention scores how much each word matters to each other word. “It” links strongly to “ball,” because balls roll. Distance in the sentence stops being a barrier.

5. Why can a language model state a fact that isn't true?

This is the big one — remember Johann Kessler and his bagel. The model builds text one likely piece at a time. It isn't checking a fact, it's producing a plausible continuation. Usually plausible and true match. Sometimes they don't, and it sounds just as confident either way.

6. A model 100 times smaller gave answers people preferred. What made the difference?

Human feedback beat being 100× bigger. Every chatbot you've used was shaped by people patiently comparing answers and saying which was better. Humans aren't outside the machine — they're inside the training loop.

7. Dot has drafted an invitation. Why should it ask Maya before sending it?

Once an agent can act, its mistakes stop being just words on a screen. Sending, buying, and deleting are hard to undo — so a person checks first. Maya chose the picnic, and Maya decides what goes out.
